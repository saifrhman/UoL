\section{Definitions}
\subsection{Problem Modelling}

The objective of this study is to predict patient diagnostic outcomes using demographic and clinical variables. The dataset contains measurements related to sleep quality, fatigue severity, stress levels, and behavioural indicators. The target variable diagnosis contains three categories: Depression, CI, and Both. Consequently, the task is formulated as a supervised multiclass classification problem.

\bigskip

The choice of classification over alternative modelling approaches is justified as follows. Regression is inappropriate because the target variable is categorical and does not possess a meaningful numerical scale or natural ordering between categories, predicting a continuous value would have no clinical interpretation in this context. Clustering is also unsuitable because the diagnostic labels are already known for all observations; unsupervised methods are intended for settings where class membership is unknown and must be inferred from the data alone. Since the goal is to assign each patient to one of three predefined diagnostic categories based on labelled training data, supervised classification is the appropriate modelling framework.

\bigskip
The dataset initially contained 670 observations and 19 variables. The aim is to determine whether the available demographic and clinical variables can accurately predict diagnostic outcomes.

\subsection{Data Analysis and Feature Selection}

Initial exploration confirmed that the dataset contains no missing values and no duplicate observations. The dataset size is relatively small to moderate, making classical machine learning models more appropriate than deep learning approaches.

Variables were classified according to their statistical meaning rather than their storage data types. Nominal variables represent unordered categories, ordinal variables possess a natural ordering, binary variables represent two-category indicators, and numerical variables were further distinguished as discrete or continuous based on their observed distributions and measurement scales. This classification guided subsequent preprocessing decisions such as encoding strategies and feature scaling.

Inspection of the dataset revealed that the \textit{id} variable represents a unique patient identifier and therefore provides no predictive value. Additionally, the \textit{country} variable was constant across all observations and was removed from the dataset.

A statistical summary of the numerical variables revealed that \textit{depression\_phq9\_score} contained a value of 99, which lies outside the valid clinical range (0--27) of the PHQ-9 depression scale. This observation was treated as an invalid entry and removed, resulting in a dataset containing 669 observations.

Several visualisation techniques were used to analyse the dataset. Histograms and violin plots were used to examine the distribution of numerical variables, while boxplots were used to identify potential outliers using the interquartile range criterion. Most variables exhibited smooth bounded distributions without evidence of extreme multimodality. Discrete variables such as \textit{depression\_phq9\_score} and \textit{pem\_duration\_hours} displayed step-like patterns consistent with integer-valued measurements.

Although some values appeared outside whisker boundaries in the boxplots, inspection confirmed that these observations remained within clinically valid ranges and were therefore retained.

Scatterplots were used to explore relationships between numerical variables and diagnosis classes. For example, fatigue severity tended to be higher for the \textit{CI} and \textit{Both} groups, whereas stress levels were distributed across all classes.

Class distribution analysis revealed moderate imbalance, with the \textit{Both} class containing fewer observations (Figure~\ref{fig:classdist}). This motivated the use of stratified sampling in the experimental protocol.

\subsection{Correlation and Redundancy Analysis}

A Pearson correlation heatmap was used to analyse relationships among numerical variables (Figure~\ref{fig:pearsonheatmap}). A very strong correlation ($r = 0.97$) was observed between \textit{sleep\_quality\_index} and \textit{deep\_sleep\_quality\_index}, indicating substantial redundancy between the two variables. Highly correlated predictors can introduce multicollinearity and provide limited additional information for many machine learning models.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{pearson_heatmap.png}
\caption{Pearson correlation heatmap of numerical variables before feature reduction}
\label{fig:pearsonheatmap}

To assess whether both variables contributed useful discriminatory information, point plots and one-way ANOVA tests were conducted across diagnosis groups. Neither variable showed statistically significant mean differences across classes. However, \textit{sleep\_quality\_index} exhibited slightly clearer group separation, and therefore \textit{deep\_sleep\_quality\_index} was removed. Aside from this pair, the remaining numerical variables showed only weak to moderate correlations.

\subsection{Categorical Association Analysis}

To examine the relationship between categorical predictors and the target variable (\textit{diagnosis}), Cramér's V statistics were computed. Cramér's V is a measure of association between categorical variables derived from the chi-square statistic and ranges from 0 (no association) to 1 (perfect association). It is commonly used to evaluate the strength of relationships between categorical variables in classification problems.

The computed Cramér's V values between each categorical predictor and the diagnosis variable are presented in Table~\ref{tab:cramersv}.

The results indicate weak associations between most categorical predictors and the diagnosis variable. For example, \textit{gender} and \textit{meditation\_or\_mindfulness} show almost no relationship with diagnosis, while \textit{work\_status}, \textit{exercise\_frequency}, and \textit{social\_activity\_level} exhibit only weak associations.

However, \textit{pem\_present} shows a perfect association with the diagnosis variable ($V = 1.000$). This indicates that the feature deterministically separates the diagnosis categories and therefore represents target leakage. Including such a variable would artificially inflate model performance. Consequently, \textit{pem\_present} was removed prior to model training.

After removing this variable, Cramér's V values among the remaining categorical predictors were negligible ($V < 0.10$), indicating no strong associations between retained features.

Overall, the feature screening process removed the identifier column, the constant feature, the invalid PHQ-9 observation, the highly redundant sleep-related variable, and the leakage-prone \textit{pem\_present} variable. The resulting feature set was considered suitable for modelling.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

To ensure reliable model evaluation, the dataset was divided into training and testing subsets using an 80/20 stratified train--test split. Stratification preserves class distribution across both subsets.

Model selection and hyperparameter tuning were performed using stratified 5-fold cross-validation on the training set. Macro F1-score was selected as the primary evaluation metric because it provides balanced performance across all classes and is robust to moderate class imbalance.

Machine learning models require numerical inputs; therefore, categorical variables were encoded prior to model training. Nominal variables such as \textit{gender} and \textit{work\_status} were encoded using one-hot encoding to avoid imposing artificial ordinal structure. Ordinal variables such as \textit{social\_activity\_level} and \textit{exercise\_frequency} were encoded using ordinal encoding that preserved their inherent ordering. Binary variables were mapped to 0/1 representation. Numerical variables were standardised for models that are sensitive to feature magnitude, such as Logistic Regression and k-Nearest Neighbours, while tree-based models were trained without scaling.

To prevent data leakage, encoding and scaling were implemented within a preprocessing pipeline and applied during cross-validation rather than to the full dataset prior to splitting.

\subsection{Selected Machine Learning Methods}

Three machine learning algorithms discussed during the lectures were evaluated: Logistic Regression, k-Nearest Neighbours (KNN), and Random Forest.

Logistic Regression provides a linear baseline classifier. KNN represents a distance-based learning approach relying on local neighbourhood similarity. Random Forest is an ensemble tree-based method capable of capturing nonlinear relationships and feature interactions. Comparing these models allows investigation of whether linear decision boundaries, local instance similarity, or nonlinear ensemble structures best explain the diagnostic outcomes.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search}

Hyperparameter tuning was performed using grid search within stratified 5-fold cross-validation. Stratification ensures that each fold preserves the class distribution of the target variable, which is important given the moderate class imbalance observed in the dataset. This approach provides a reliable estimate of model performance while preventing overfitting to a single validation split. \section{Results}
\subsection{Decisions Based on Data Analysis}

Exploratory data analysis guided several preprocessing decisions. The identifier column \textit{id} was removed because it does not contain predictive information. The variable \textit{country} was removed as it was constant across all observations. An invalid value in \textit{depression\_phq9\_score} (99) was identified and removed based on the known clinical range of the PHQ-9 scale.

Correlation analysis revealed a strong relationship between \textit{sleep\_quality\_index} and \textit{deep\_sleep\_quality\_index}, and the latter was removed to reduce redundancy. Cramér's V analysis identified \textit{pem\_present} as a leakage variable with perfect association with the target variable, and it was therefore removed to prevent artificially inflated model performance.

The hyperparameter search results are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparameters}. Logistic Regression achieved a Macro F1-score of 0.774 with $C=10$, indicating that weaker regularisation improved performance. 

The KNN model achieved a lower score of 0.548 with $k=11$, suggesting that local neighbourhood similarity alone was insufficient to separate the classes effectively. Additionally, the feature space in this dataset is heterogeneous, containing a mix of numerical and encoded categorical variables with different scales and distributions, which further distorts distance calculations. The relatively large optimal k = 11 also suggests that the decision boundaries are not well-captured by local neighbourhood structure alone.

Random Forest achieved the highest cross-validated Macro F1-score of 0.947 with $n\_estimators=500$ and $max\_depth=10$. This indicates that nonlinear relationships and feature interactions play an important role in predicting diagnosis. Consequently, Random Forest was selected for final evaluation on the test set.

Logistic Regression achieved moderate performance (0.774), confirming that some linear structure is present but insufficient on its own. The consistently weak performance of KNN (0.548) across all tested values of k further suggests that distance-based reasoning does not align well with the structure of this particular feature space.
\bigbreak
These results highlight an important consideration for clinical prediction tasks: when the relationship between predictors and outcomes is complex and involves interactions between multiple variables, ensemble methods such as Random Forest tend to outperform simpler models. Based on these findings, Random Forest was selected as the final candidate model.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

This study evaluated machine learning methods for predicting diagnostic outcomes using demographic and clinical variables. Exploratory data analysis and feature screening were used to remove redundant and leakage-prone variables before modelling.

Among the evaluated methods, Random Forest achieved the best performance, indicating that nonlinear relationships and feature interactions are important for this task. The final model demonstrated strong performance on the held-out test set, suggesting good generalisation.

Future work could explore additional ensemble methods, alternative feature engineering strategies, or larger datasets to further improve predictive performance.
